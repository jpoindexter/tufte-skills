# PowerPoint and the Corruption of Evidence

## Overview

PowerPoint does not merely inconvenience presenters — it actively degrades the analytical quality of the information passing through it. Tufte's chapter "The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint: Pitching Out Corrupts Within" in *Beautiful Evidence* documents this through controlled comparisons, a body count (the Columbia shuttle), and a word-by-word dissection of what happens when bullets replace sentences. The core mechanism is foreshortening: PP's default cognitive style makes complex ideas look structurally identical to simple ones, strips causal relationships from evidence, and substitutes hierarchy for reasoning. The alternative is not better slides — it is written reports, sentences, and paper handouts.

---

## §1. The PP Cognitive Style: A Named Defect List

Tufte defines the "cognitive style characteristic of the standard default PP presentation" as a compound of specific, named defects — not aesthetic preferences, but structural failures in evidence communication.

| Defect | What it does to evidence |
|---|---|
| Foreshortening | Compresses complex reasoning to match simple reasoning; all ideas appear equally important |
| Low spatial resolution | Median 40 words per slide — 8 seconds of silent reading material |
| Single-path hierarchy | One nested outline per slide forces evidence into one route through an argument |
| Narrative fragmentation | Data and reasoning broken across slides; context cannot span the break |
| Rapid temporal sequencing | Information appears and vanishes before comparison is possible |
| Chartjunk and Phluff | 40–60% of slide space devoted to bullets, frames, branding, clip art — not data |
| Presenter-orientation | PP advertising: "A cure for the presentation jitters." Content is not the product |
| Incompetent table/graph defaults | PP default graphics templates corrupt statistical data (see §5) |

> "The cognitive style characteristic of the standard default PP presentation: foreshortening of evidence and thought, low spatial resolution, an intensely hierarchical single-path structure as the model for organizing every type of content, breaking up narratives and data into slides and minimal fragments, rapid temporal sequencing of thin information rather than focused spatial analysis, conspicuous chartjunk and PP Phluff, branding of slides with logotypes, a preoccupation with format not content, incompetent designs for data graphics and tables, and a smirky commercialism that turns information into a sales pitch and presenters into marketeers."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 158

### The distribution of who PP helps

- Bottom 10% of presenters: PP forces them to have *some* points
- Top 10% of presenters: strong content and personal style neutralizes PP damage
- **80% of presenters: PP cognitive style causes trouble**

## §2. Columbia: The Case Study

### What happened

On January 16, 2003, 82 seconds after liftoff, a 1.67-pound (760g) piece of foam insulation broke off the liquid fuel tank, hit the left wing at approximately 600 mph relative to the shuttle (kinetic energy scales as velocity squared), and created a hole in the thermal protection system. Columbia burned up on re-entry 16 days later. All 7 astronauts died.

Boeing Corporation engineers prepared 3 PowerPoint reports — 28 slides — to help NASA officials assess the threat while Columbia was still in orbit. Those slides, and the cognitive style that shaped them, were examined by both the Columbia Accident Investigation Board (CAIB) and the 2005 Return to Flight Task Group.

### The key slide: anatomy of a failure

The Boeing slide titled **"Review of Test Data Indicates Conservatism for Tile Penetration"** contains:

- 6 levels of hierarchy to classify 11 phrases
- The "Very Big Bullet" (VBB) fragment at the top that does not make sense on its own
- The word "significant" used **5 times** with 5 different meanings (ranging from "detectable in a perhaps irrelevant calibration case study" to "an amount of damage so that everyone dies")
- A critical number — **1920 cu. in. vs. 3 cu. in.** — showing the debris was **640 times larger** than data used in the model, buried at the fourth level of indentation in the final bullet of the slide
- Three different typographic representations of the same unit (cubic inches) on one slide: `3cu. In`, `1920cu in`, `3 cu in for test`

**What the title says:** "Test Data Indicates Conservatism for Tile Penetration" (reassuring)

**What the title should say:** "Review of Test Data Indicates Irrelevance of Two Models" (accurate)

> "The analysis begins with the dreaded Executive Summary, with a conclusion presented as a headline: 'Test Data Indicates Conservatism for Tile Penetration.' This turns out to be unmerited reassurance."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 164

### The organizational failure PP enabled

> "The Mission Management Team Chair's position in the hierarchy governed what information she would or would not receive. Information was lost as it traveled up the hierarchy. A demoralized Debris Assessment Team did not include a slide about the need for better imagery in their presentation to the Mission Evaluation Room. Their presentation included the Crater analysis, which they reported as incomplete and uncertain. However, the Mission Evaluation Room manager perceived the Boeing analysis as rigorous and quantitative. The choice of headings, arrangement of information, and size of bullets on the key chart served to highlight what management already believed. The uncertainties and assumptions that signaled danger dropped out of the information chain..."
> — Columbia Accident Investigation Board, *Report*, vol. 1 (August 2003), p. 201, quoted in *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 166

### CAIB's verdict on PP at NASA

> "The Board views the endemic use of PowerPoint briefing slides instead of technical papers as an illustration of the problematic methods of technical communication at NASA."
> — Columbia Accident Investigation Board, *Report*, vol. 1 (August 2003), p. 191, quoted in *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 167

### Lower-level engineers did it right

While management was reading PP decks, lower-level NASA engineers wrote hundreds of emails about the Columbia threat. Tufte's analysis of those emails:

- 90% "simply used *sentences* sequentially ordered into *paragraphs*"
- 10% used bullet lists with 2 or 3 levels
- These engineers were able to reason about the issues without employing hierarchical outlines

> "The *language, attitude, and presentation tool of the pitch culture had penetrated throughout the NASA organization, even into the most serious technical work, a real-time engineering analysis of threats to the survival of the shuttle.*"
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 166

### What both review boards concluded

Both the CAIB and the Return to Flight Task Group — after examining hundreds of PP decks from a high-IQ government agency with rocket scientists as authors — concluded:

1. PowerPoint is an **inappropriate tool** for engineering reports, presentations, and documentation
2. The **technical report is superior** to PP

## §3. Bullet Lists Fragment Reasoning

### What bullets can and cannot express

A bullet list can communicate exactly **3 logical relationships**:

| Relationship | Example |
|---|---|
| Sequence | First to last in time |
| Priority | Least to most important (or vice versa) |
| Simple membership | These items relate in some way (nature unstated) |

A list can show **only one** of those relationships at a time. Causality, conditionality, comparison, evidence-weighting, and uncertainty — the relationships that matter in analytical work — are inexpressible in bullet form.

### The Harvard Business Review finding

Shaw, Brown, and Bromiley (HBR, 76, May–June 1998) studying business planning:

> "Bullet lists are typically too generic. They offer a series of things to do that could apply to any business.... Bullets leave critical relationships unspecified. Lists can communicate only three logical relationships: sequence (first to last in time); priority (least to most important or vice versa); or simple membership in a set (these items relate to one another in some way, but the nature of that relationship remains unstated)."
> — quoted in *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 170

They also found bullets leave "critical assumptions about how the business works unstated" and "displace narratives, an effective tool for thinking and for presentations."

### The Feynman rule

Richard Feynman experienced PP-style bullets while investigating the 1986 Challenger accident:

> "Then we learned about 'bullets' — little black circles in front of phrases that were supposed to summarize things. There was one after another of these little goddamn bullets in our briefing books and on slides."
> — Richard P. Feynman, *What Do You Care What Other People Think?* (New York, 1988), 126–127, quoted in *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 171

Feynman wrote about much of physics — classical mechanics through quantum electrodynamics — in 3 textbook volumes totaling 1,800 pages. **Those books use no bullets and only 2 levels of hierarchy** (chapters and subheads within chapters).

### The sentence is the unit of thought

> "It is thoughtless and arrogant to replace the sentence as the basic unit for explaining something. Especially as the byproduct of some marketing presentation software."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 170

**Do/Don't pair — unit of thought:**

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Write full sentences with subjects, predicates, nouns, verbs — which combine sequentially to form paragraphs | Reduce claims to noun-phrase fragments in indented lists |
| State the logical relationship explicitly: "because," "therefore," "however," "compared to" | Leave the relationship implied by bullet position |
| Allow complexity to require length | Force length to fit slide format |

### Bullets and hierarchy scale inversely with analytical quality

> "As analysis becomes more causal, multivariate, comparative, evidence-based, and resolution-intense, the more damaging the bullet list becomes."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 171

## §4. Information Density: The Quantitative Case Against PP

### Words per slide (measured data)

| Source | Median words per slide/page |
|---|---|
| 26 Columbia reports by Boeing | 97 |
| 1,460 text-only slides in 189 PP reports (internet, 2003) | 40 |
| 654 slides in 28 PP textbooks (1997–2003) | 15 |

40 words = approximately 8 seconds of silent reading material. 15 words approaches billboard-level communication.

### Characters per page and information density

| Format | Characters per page | Density (char/in²) |
|---|---|---|
| Physicians' Desk Reference | 13,600 | 168 |
| Your Income Tax | 10,400 | 118 |
| World Almanac | 9,800 | 232 |
| Merck Manual | 4,700 | 117 |
| Google News (web, 2003) | 4,100 | 44 |
| New York Times (web) | 4,100 | 43 |
| **Columbia PP reports by Boeing** | **630** | **7** |
| **1,460 PP text slides (internet)** | **250** | **3** |
| **654 PP textbook slides** | **98** | **1** |
| Content-free slides | 0 | 0 |

> "The PP slide format has the worst signal/noise ratio of any known method of communication on paper or computer screen."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 180

A single page of *Physicians' Desk Reference* equals 54 typical PP slides. A nonfiction bestseller delivers information at **10 to 50 times** the density of a printed PP deck.

### The design style wastes space

PP's design style uses **40% to 60% of available slide space** for:
- Phluff (bullets, frames, background graphics)
- Branding and logotypes
- Slide titles repeated across every slide
- Hierarchical indentation structure

The remaining space holds the actual content.

## §5. Statistical Data and PP Templates

### The cancer survival rate demonstration

196 numbers and 57 words describe survival rates and standard errors for 24 cancers — a single compact table with strong comparative structure. Applying PP default graphics templates to this data produces:

- **6 separate chaotic slides**
- Consuming **2.9 times** the area of the original table
- Uncomparative, thin data-density, chartjunk, encoded legends, meaningless color, logotype branding
- Each slide shows only 4 cancers — cross-cancer comparison impossible
- Time-trend comparison across the 4 time points is obscured

> "Everything is wrong with these smarmy, incoherent graphs: uncomparative, thin data-density, chartjunk, encoded legends, meaningless color, logotype branding, indifference to content and evidence. Chartjunk is a clear sign of statistical stupidity; use these designs in your presentation, and your audience will quickly and correctly conclude that you don't know much about data and evidence."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 174

### Median entries in data matrices for statistical graphics (2003)

| Publication | Median entries |
|---|---|
| Science | > 1,000 |
| Nature | > 700 |
| New York Times | 120 |
| Wall Street Journal | 112 |
| New England Journal of Medicine | 53 |
| Financial Times | 40 |
| The Economist | 32 |
| **28 books on PP presentations (1997–2003)** | **12** |
| Pravda (1982, Soviet propaganda) | 5 |

PP books beat Pravda. That is the benchmark.

## §6. The Foreshortening Problem

Foreshortening is Tufte's term for PP's most dangerous structural effect: the format makes all ideas look equally important because every idea gets the same treatment — a bullet, an indent level, a slide. There is no typographic or structural mechanism for expressing "this is the critical finding" vs. "this is a supporting detail" except position in a list.

**In the Boeing Columbia slide:**
- The reassuring executive summary (Level 1) gets the largest type and most prominence
- The damning number — debris 640x larger than test data — is at Level 4, in the smallest type, in the final bullet, at the bottom of the slide
- Management read the headline and the conclusion. The critical number was invisible to the cognitive apparatus that hierarchical bullet structure creates.

### The foreshortening failure pattern

1. Important claim: stated as headline (Level 1) — seen
2. Supporting evidence: Level 2–3 bullets — skimmed
3. Disconfirming data, uncertainty flags, scope limitations: Level 4–6 — missed
4. Conclusion: executive summary restates Level 1 — "confirmed"

### Do/Don't pair — information weight:

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Put the most important finding in the most prominent position with explicit signal: "The critical result is..." | Let position in a hierarchy do the work of signaling importance |
| State scope limitations and uncertainties in the same sentence as the claim | Bury exceptions at lower indent levels where they become invisible to hierarchy-reading managers |
| Use the sentence to hold the relationship between claim and evidence together | Fragment claim and evidence across separate bullets |

## §7. Stylesheets, Templates, and Conway's Law

PP's cognitive style is not random — it is the cognitive style of a large software corporation engaged in computer programming (deep hierarchical structures) and marketing (advocacy not analysis, slogans, branding). Conway's Law: "Any organization which designs a system will inevitably produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization's communication structure."

The PP metaphor is the software corporation itself. The results:
- Corporate and government agencies mandate designated PP stylesheets
- Stylesheets enforce the limitations of PP on every presenter
- Templates are designed to the level of reading primers for 6-year-olds (see: Harvard School of Public Health's "6x6 rule" — 6 lines, 6 words per line)

> "These templates are uninformed by the practices of scientific publication and the rich intellectual history of evidence and analysis in public health. The templates do, however, emulate the format of reading primers for 6 year-olds."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 177

The Gettysburg Address in PP (Peter Norvig parody): Lincoln's "four score and seven years ago" becomes "-87 years" on an x-axis. "Government of the people, by the people, for the people" becomes three separate bullets under "Gov't of/for/by the people."

## §8. Named Failure Modes

These are specific, named failure modes from Tufte's analysis of the Boeing Columbia slides and PP in general. Check each before publishing a PP-based report.

| Failure mode | Description | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **VBB Fragment** | Very Big Bullet phrase does not make sense as a complete thought | Lead bullet requires the slide title to parse |
| **Headline Reassurance** | Executive summary title states a conclusion the body evidence does not support | Title is optimistic; lower bullets contain uncertainty or contradictions |
| **Significance Dilution** | A vague quantitative word ("significant") used multiple times with different unstated meanings | Count uses of "significant," "substantial," "notable" per slide |
| **Foreshortened Hierarchy** | Critical disconfirming data buried at level 4+ | Scan level 4+ bullets first; if any are more important than level 1, the structure is inverted |
| **Unit Inconsistency** | Same measurement expressed in different notation on one slide | Multiple representations of the same unit (3cu. In / 1920cu in / 3 cu in) |
| **Orphan Numbers** | Isolated numbers that cannot be compared to anything else | Numbers without a reference class, baseline, or range |
| **Scope Concealment** | The slide title implies the analysis covers more than the bullets actually address | "Analysis of X" when the analysis only addresses one sub-aspect of X |
| **PP Phluff** | Space devoted to formatting, branding, decoration rather than data | > 40% of slide area contains no information |
| **Chartjunk Graphics** | Default PP graph templates applied to data that would be clearer as a table | Any 3D bar chart; any chart with encoded color legends for small datasets |
| **Temporal Fragmentation** | Related evidence split across multiple slides so comparison requires memory | Data that belongs in one eyespan distributed across 3+ slides |

## §9. What to Use Instead

### The hierarchy of alternatives

1. **Technical report or scientific paper** — the format used in Nature and Science. Narrative paragraphs, full sentences, comparative tables, publication-quality statistical graphics.

2. **The 4-pager** — A3, 30 by 42 cm (11 by 17 inches folded to 4 pages). One piece of paper can show:
   - Up to 60,000 characters of words and numbers
   - Detailed tables with hundreds of numbers
   - 1,000 sparkline statistical graphics showing 500,000 numbers
   - **Content-equivalent of 50 to 250 typical PP slides**

3. **Paper handouts at talks** — allow viewers to contextualize, compare, narrate, and recast evidence. Readers can process information 3x faster than a presenter can speak.

4. **Word-processing software** — forces sentences. Sentences force presenters to be smarter. "Writing sentences forces presenters to be smarter. And presentations based on sentences make consumers smarter as well."

### What PP is legitimately good for

- Full-screen projected images and videos
- Talking points at informal meetings (where they stay informal)
- Slide manager for talks that are primarily visual

> "Meetings should center on concisely written reports on paper, not fragmented bulleted talking points projected up on the wall. A good model for the technical report is a scientific paper or commentary on a paper published in substantial scientific journals such as Nature or Science."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 168

> "Serious problems require a serious tool: written reports. For nearly all engineering and scientific communication, instead of PowerPoint, *the presentation and reporting software should be a word-processing program* capable of capturing, editing, and publishing text, tables, data graphics, images, and scientific notation."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 168

### Do/Don't pair — serious technical presentations:

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Distribute a concise briefing paper; let the audience read for 5–10 minutes before speaking | Open with a title slide and read bullets aloud |
| Use the written report as the permanent record; use talk for guided analysis and Q&A | Treat the PP deck as the documentation |
| Provide paper handouts with full data tables and graphics at full resolution | Project thin data at low resolution from 15+ feet away |
| Cite primary sources and show the actual data | Summarize summaries |

## §10. The Orwell Principle

> "PowerPoint becomes ugly and inaccurate because our thoughts are foolish, but the slovenliness of PowerPoint makes it easier for us to have foolish thoughts."
> — *Beautiful Evidence*, p. 181, applying Orwell's principle from "Politics and the English Language"

The causal arrow runs both directions. PP promotes a cognitive style that makes it easier to think in fragments, hierarchy, and slogans. Thinking in fragments, hierarchy, and slogans makes PP look adequate. Escaping the loop requires changing the tool.

**Competitive result:** In Tufte's controlled comparisons across 10 case studies and 32 control samples, PP was outperformed by alternative presentation tools on every measure — except beating Pravda in statistical graphics. Technical reports are smarter than PP. Sentences are smarter than bullet points. The Feynman principle applies: if the format cannot handle the physics, don't use the format for the physics.
